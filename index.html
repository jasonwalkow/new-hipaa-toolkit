Overview

The Health Insurance Portability and Accountability ACT (HIPAA) is a broad set of regulations that address, among other things, the permissible uses and disclosure of protected health information (PHI).

Does my organization need to be HIPAA Compliant?

Your organization is required to be HIPAA-compliant if it is either a covered entity or a business associate.

How do I know if my organization is a covered entity or a business associate?

A covered entity is either a health plan, a healthcare clearinghouse, or a healthcare provider.

Health plans and healthcare clearinghouses are fairly specialized entities. If your organization is one of those, you probably already know it. However, most organizations which have HIPAA obligations fall into the category of healthcare providers.

How do I know if my organization is a healthcare provider?

The term “healthcare provider” covers a broad range of organizations providing direct care, as well as a variety of health-related services, including: Physicians, Surgeons, Dentists, Podiatrists, Laboratory Technicians, Optometrists, Hospitals, Clinics, Nursing homes, and Pharmacies.

Well, my organization isn’t a healthcare provider, but you said that my organization might also be required to be HIPAA-compliant if it is a business associate.

How can I tell if my organization is a business associate?

A business associate is any organization or individual who acts as a vendor or subcontractor with access to PHI. Examples of business associates include: Data transmission providers, data processing firms, data storage or document shredding companies, medical equipment and device companies, consultants hired for audits or coding reviews, electronic health information exchanges, medical transcription services, external auditors or accountants, and health insurance brokerages.

Okay, so I now know that my organization is either a covered entity or business associate, but how do I know whether we handle PHI?

What is PHI?

Any information included in a medical record that can identify an individual and was created and used while providing health care (such as diagnosis or treatment) is considered PHI. PHI can also consist of records of conversations a patient has with a physician or nurse about his or her treatment, a patient’s billing information, or medical information in the patient’s health insurance company’s database.

But we still use only paper medical records at our organization, aren’t we exempt from HIPAA?

No. Even if your organization is still using paper medical records, when you submit claims in hard copy to a billing company, and that company in turn transmits those records electronically to payers, HIPAA’s requirements apply to your organization as well.
